Essential Safety Equipment


Resort Slope Protection



Armor Up for the Slopes

MIPS Helmet
A standard helmet is great, but MIPS adds a special sliding layer that reduces twisting forces on your brain if you fall at a weird angle.
Evo REI


Spine & Back Protector
Great for park riders and fast skiers. A back protector takes the heavy hit if you fall backward onto ice or hidden rocks, saving your spine from serious damage.
Amazon Evo


Impact Shorts
Padded shorts protect your tailbone and hips. Highly recommended if you are learning to snowboard or practicing new tricks in the terrain park.
Amazon Evo


Wrist Guards
Great for snowboarders or anyone who tends to stick their hands out to catch their fall. This prevents the most common broken bones on icy runs.
Amazon Evo




Backcountry & Out of Bounds Gear



Surviving the Deep Snow

Avalanche Transceiver (Beacon)
A critical device that sends out a beep. If you are buried in the snow, your partners switch theirs to search mode to find your exact location. Required for skiing outside resort boundaries.
Evo


Avalanche Probe
A long, folding metal stick used to poke into the snow so you can pinpoint exactly where a buried skier is before you start digging.
REI


Snow Shovel
A strong metal shovel (never plastic) is needed to dig out a buried friend. Snow that falls in an avalanche sets up like concrete, so you need strong gear.
Evo


Airbag Backpack
Pulling a handle inflates a massive balloon behind your head. This large balloon helps you float to the top of the moving snow if you are caught in an avalanche.
Evo






The 3 Pillars of Mountain Education



Having the right gear is only half the battle. Explore the three essential types of training every skier and snowboarder should understand.

1. Resort Slope Rules




The mountain is shared by everyone, from absolute beginners to advanced experts. Understanding the basic mountain rules is your first step to staying safe. This includes knowing who has the right of way (always the person downhill from you), how to merge safely onto a trail, and where to stop without hiding behind a blind corner. Knowing these basic rules stops crashes from happening.

2. Avalanche Safety Classes




If you plan to ski outside the marked resort boundaries, taking an introductory avalanche safety class is absolutely required. These hands on courses teach you how to read the mountain, understand if the snow is safe, and make sense of daily avalanche reports. Most importantly, you will learn how to use your beacon, probe, and shovel to quickly rescue your friends if things go wrong.

3. Wilderness First Aid




Mountain environments are remote, freezing, and unforgiving. If someone in your group breaks a leg or starts freezing too fast, ski patrol might be hours away depending on where you are. A Wilderness First Aid class teaches you how to treat serious injuries, build emergency leg splints out of ski poles, and keep a person warm until a rescue helicopter arrives. It is the ultimate backup plan.
